Let Siri do the tapping.

Talking beats hunting for icons — easier on shaky hands, tired eyes, and short memories. Switch it on, teach three phrases, and let them ask out loud.

Set Siri up once

Switch Siri on.

Settings → Siri → Listen for ‘Hey Siri’ → on.

Train it to their voice.

Walk through the setup phrases it asks for.

Save their people in Contacts.

“Call my daughter” needs a daughter saved.

Two ways to summon it

Say “Hey Siri.”

Hands-free — best for shaky hands.

Or hold the side button.

Better in noisy rooms, or if “Hey Siri” misfires.

Teach the phrases that matter

Pick three they’ll use daily.

Calling, reminders, timers — not everything at once.

Write their exact phrases down.

Their daughter’s name; their pill time.

Leave them the large-print card.

The companion to this poster.

Have them say one out loud now.

Saying it once beats watching you.

What it’s great for

Calling and FaceTime.

“Call Robert,” “FaceTime my son” — no scrolling.

Remembering things.

“Remind me to take my pills at 8.”

Timers and alarms.

“Set a timer for 20 minutes.”

Quick questions.

The weather, the time, a word, a fact.

Help them talk to it

Speak normally — no shouting.

Siri hears a calm voice just fine.

Wait a beat after “Hey Siri.”

Let the chime sound, then ask.

If it misses, just try again.

No harm in a second go.

When it goes sideways

“I didn’t catch that.”

Rephrase shorter, or use the side button.

Wrong person called?

Check the name is saved the way they say it.

Helping elders keep their independence — and giving their helpers the tools to do it well.
